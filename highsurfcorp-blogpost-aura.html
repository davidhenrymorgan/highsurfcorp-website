Protecting Your
Shoreline.

Alexander Vane

Lead Structural Engineer

Figure 1.1 — Coquina revetment installation along the eastern barrier island coast.

The boundary between land and sea is not a line, but a negotiation.

For decades, property owners have relied on rigid vertical structures to define this boundary. But as tides shift and storm surges intensify, the philosophy of resistance is giving way to a more sophisticated approach: dissipation.

In Brevard County, the unique geological composition of the shoreline demands a specific engineering response. The standard concrete seawall, while effective in calm waters, often creates a "scouring" effect during high-energy events. The wave energy hits the vertical face, reflects downward, and excavates the sand at the toe of the wall. Over time, this undermines the very structure meant to protect the land.

The Return to Roughness

Engineering satisfaction today comes from texture. By introducing coquina revetments—sloped structures made of irregular native limestone—we change the physics of the wave impact. Instead of reflecting energy, the porous nature of the rock absorbs it. The water rushes into the voids between the stones, losing velocity and force before it can retreat.

"We are not building walls to stop the ocean. We are building systems to converse with it."

This method, known as "living shorelines" in some circles, is more than an ecological preference; it is a structural imperative for longevity. A rigid wall creates a binary failure state: it is either standing, or it has failed. A revetment, however, is adaptive. It can settle, shift slightly, and remain functional.

Material Selection

Not all stone is equal. We source high-density coquina from deep inland quarries, ensuring a calcium carbonate content that naturally cements over time. When placed in the intertidal zone, this rock becomes a substrate for marine life—oysters, barnacles, and algae—which essentially creates a "biocrete" layer, further armoring the shoreline against erosion.

The lifespan of these installations exceeds traditional concrete by nearly double, assuming proper maintenance. While the initial footprint is larger—requiring a slope rather than a vertical cut—the reduction in hydrostatic pressure behind the barrier protects the uplands from the sinkholes common behind aging sea walls.

Protecting your legacy requires looking beyond the next hurricane season and envisioning the coastline of the next century. It requires materials that don't just resist nature, but emulate its most durable forms.
